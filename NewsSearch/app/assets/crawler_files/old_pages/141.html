Critically endangered rhino dies at Safari Park

Posted: Updated:

SAN DIEGO (CBS8/AP) — A northern white rhinoceros that zoo officials said was only one of six left in the world died Sunday at the San Diego Zoo Safari Park.

Angalifu, who was about 44 years old, apparently died of old age.

The death of the popular white rhino is sparking a conversation about the global poaching problem.

Conversation experts say that if nothing is done soon, the northern white rhinoceros could become extinct.

Angalifu was a popular animal for many of the visitors at the San Diego Safari Park.

“He was a gorgeous animal and he was very well known at the Safari Park and I know everyone that's been working with the species and working with him is really missing him” says Christina Simmons, San Diego Zoo's public relations manager.

Park officials discovered that he had passed away while under veterinary care early Sunday morning.

“Angalifu was 44 years old, which is very old for rhinos, although we don't have the full results of the post mortem and we don't expect to have them for a little bit we expect it will show he died of natural causes” says Christina.

His death is highlighting the serious problems poaching poses to endangered species.

"The loss of Angalifu just brings this species closer to extinction."

White rhinos have been decimated as a result of poaching, so beginning in the early 90's the San Diego Zoo became part of a program aimed at saving the species.

At one point, the zoo had three white rhinos but they were unable to get them to breed.

“What is fueling poaching is the tremendous black market in horn, elephant ivory and other animal parts.”

Currently, there are only five northern white rhinos left.

Four females and one male.

“We will continue to hold out hope that there will be some kind of scientific breakthrough that allows us to come up with a way to save the species, but we don't know of any way at this point that will do that” says Christina Simmons.

Park officials say they hope that his story inspires people to act because if things don't change, other species could face similar fates.

Experts say that rhino horn is becoming more lucrative than drugs.

That's because they sell for tens of thousands of dollars.

The demand is driven primarily by buyers in East Asia who believe it cures a series of ailments
