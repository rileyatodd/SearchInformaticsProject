The tallest towers never completed

For as long as we've been erecting buildings, our ambitions have occasionally outstripped our resources, or circumstances have intervened to upset our plans.

"Conceiving, financing, designing, and constructing a skyscraper is no simple feat, even under the best of conditions," the Journal of the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat noted in a recent report. "Many projects have had torturously slow gestation periods; many more have failed to start or were interrupted and cancelled."

The journal rounded up the tallest buildings where construction stopped. Many fell victim to the global financial meltdown in 2008, with others ran up against the Great Depression and even World War II. Some are just holes in the ground, while others rose some ways into the sky.

We've put what images we could find of the projects above. While we refer to most of them in the past tense, we recognize that some could yet re-emerge.

As the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat put it: "Rumors of resurrections persist, and today's long shot is tomorrow's sure thing."

  • Contemporary Port Townsend home dates to 1906;
  • Reimagined Tudor in Magnolia;
  • Greenwood early 20th century homes for $449,000 to $499,000.
