Jed Lowrie back with Astros on 3-year deal

Posted on December 15, 2014 | By Evan Drellich

Share this:

  • Print
  • —

Jed Lowrie, the shortstop who returned to the Astros Monday on a three-year deal worth a guaranteed $23 million, sat down with manager A.J. Hinch for lunch in Houston a couple of weeks ago. Over sushi, the two Stanford attendees discussed preparedness, a buzzword that will come up more and more as Hinch implements his philosophy this spring training, in his first year running the club.

“I think Jed brings a lot to a team,” Hinch said. “What really struck me was how he prepares for games. What he puts into every night and how he wants to win. And that matches up perfectly with how we’re evolving here in Houston, and I look forward to seeing him at shortstop.”

Many in Houston have seen him there before. Lowrie, 30, was the Astros shortstop in 2012, before being traded to the Oakland Athletics for designated hitter Chris Carter, pitcher Brad Peacock and catcher Max Stassi the following offseason. Those three remain with the Astros.

Lowrie counts as the third significant free-agent acquisition the Astros have made in a span of a week, after they agreed to terms on the night of Dec. 9 with two free agent pitchers, Luke Gregerson and Pat Neshek. Those two were introduced at Minute Maid Park on Friday, and Lowrie is set for his homecoming in front of the media at 10 a.m. Tuesday. Between the three players, owner Jim Crane’s group has committed $19.5 million in additional salary for 2015.

“I sat down with A.J. Hinch a couple weeks ago and had the opportunity to talk about (his) approach to the game, his philosophy,” Lowrie said. “I think that really helped, and I think it was the best deal as a total package. And I think it’s an exciting time to be an Astro: a lot of young talent. Now they’ve added some veteran presence, and we’ll see where they’ll go from here.”

“This is what we prioritized, which is improving our infield offense,” general manager Jeff Luhnow said. “Jed has played here. We’re comfortable with him, he’s comfortable with us. And it’s just a good fit all around.”

Correa isn’t blocked

The Astros have a full 40-man roster and said they would announce their move to make room for Lowrie on Tuesday.

A switch-hitter who still owns a home in Houston, Lowrie was listening to clubs that wanted him to play third base or second base. But his preference is to play shortstop for as long as he can, and that lined up with the ‘Stros. While third baseman Matt Dominguez did struggle in 2015, the Astros prefer Lowrie at shortstop.

The Astros have a top shortstop prospect coming to the big leagues likely sometime later in 2015 in Carlos Correa. Lowrie, perhaps, could move to third base for the following season if Correa is ready to take over. GM Jeff Luhnow said Monday that Correa is not switching positions.

If third-base prospects Colin Moran or Rio Ruiz subsequently knock on the door of the big leagues while Lowrie is with the Astros — which they are expected to — the Astros will have a more complicated decision to make with their positioning, but that’s down the road.

What would help most any player is consistency. If the club asks Lowrie to prepare for the season as a shortstop, using him at third base on Tuesdays and Sundays and shortstop Mondays and Wednesdays won’t help him. It all comes back to that buzzword: preparation.

Always on base

Lowrie’s best season came in 2013 for the A’s, when a .290 average, .344 on-base percentage and career-high 154 games also included 15 home runs. He had a lower average, .244, in 97 games for the Astros the year prior, but had a comparable OBP, 331.

In 2014 with the A’s, Lowrie hit .249 with a .321 OBP and 15 homers.

Lowrie’s deal could be worth up to $28 million if a team option for a fourth year, 2018, is exercised. His contract calls for $8 million in 2015, $7.5 million in 2016, $6.5 million in 2017. The Astros can choose between a club option of $6 million or a $1 million buyout in ’18.

There’s still work to be done for the Astros, even if there isn’t a large amount of money to be spent. Starting pitching is the next frontier, Luhnow said, with one spot in the rotation open.

Lowrie’s arrival signals that one or more of the incumbent middle infielders — Jonathan Villar, Marwin Gonzalez and Gregorio Petit — are on their way out. A person familiar with the Astros’ thinking said the club has recently been trying to trade some of the players at the bottom of their 40-man roster.

The Astros also have an unresolved logjam at catcher, with Jason Castro, Hank Conger and Carlos Corporan totaling one backstop too many.

No bad blood

While with the A’s, Lowrie had a well-publicized flap with the Astros and then-manager Bo Porter last April after bunting with the A’s ahead 7-0 early in the game. Astros pitcher Paul Clemens threw at Lowrie, too, but both the manager and pitcher are with a new organization now.

“It wasn’t anything personal,” Luhnow said. “It was a reaction to a situation. Even after that happened, I talked to Jed, Castro talked to Jed. We moved past it pretty quickly after it happened.”

ODDS AND ENDS

The Astros signed first baseman Dan Johnson to a minor league deal, according to Sportsnet’s Shi Davidi. … Starting pitcher Brett Anderson, who has a home in Houston, signed with the Dodgers on Monday. The Astros had expressed interest in him earlier this offseason.
