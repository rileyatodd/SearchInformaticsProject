Union sues NFL, seeks Adrian Peterson's immediate reinstatement

  • Article by: PAUL WALSH , Star Tribune
  • Updated: December 15, 2014 - 1:32 PM

The NFLPA followed through on its pledge and sued the league, which suspended Peterson until next spring for whipping his child with a tree branch. An arbitrator ruled in favor of the suspension on Friday.

The players union Monday made good on its pledge and sued the NFL on behalf of Minnesota Vikings star running back Adrian Peterson, seeking his immediate reinstatement from his season-ending suspension for whipping his young son.

The filing by the NFL Players Association (NFLPA) in U.S. District Court in Minneapolis petitions the court to vacate Friday’s ruling against Peterson by Harold Henderson, former NFL executive vice president for labor relations and the arbitrator appointed by NFL Commissioner Roger Goodell.

Henderson ruled that Peterson must remain away from the league and all team activities until at least April 15. Peterson will be eligible to return then if he fulfills requirements set by Goodell on Nov. 18, when he suspended Peterson for the rest of the season.

The league’s 2012 Most Valuable Player is forfeiting six game checks from the 2014 season, costing him $4.1 million from his $11.75 million salary. He has played in just one game this season, was inactive for a second, then went on the Commissioner’s Exempt List, with pay, for eight games.

Immediately after the arbitrator upheld the suspension, the NFLPA said it was considering “immediate legal remedies.”

“Mr. Peterson is entitled immediately to be reinstated as a player in the National Football League because he has already served far more than the maximum two-game suspension that could have been imposed under [the collective bargaining agreement],” the union said in the court filing.

The union’s filing argued that Henderson’s ruling “defies fundamental principles of notice, fairness and consistency; and it was rendered by an evidently partial arbitrator who exceeded the scope of his authority.”

Specifically, the court petition branded the suspension and arbitrator’s ruling as “retroactive punishment” of Peterson under the league’s revised and more stringent discipline procedures.

Had Peterson been punished under the league policies in force in May, when he struck his son, “he could not lawfully have been suspended for more than two games,” the petition continued.

The petition alleged that Goodell was “apparently motived by public calls for his resignation due to disciplinary failures” in other cases such as the domestic abuse incident involving running back Ray Rice, who did win reinstatement.

The union petition also accused Goodell of choosing a former league executive as the arbitrator in Peterson’s appeal to “insulate his own actions from any type of meaningful review.” As expected, the union continued, Henderson “rubber-stamped the unlawful process and punishment of Mr. Peterson.”

Peterson, 29, was suspended by Goodell on Nov. 18 following his no contest plea in Texas to a misdemeanor charge of reckless assault. He had been indicted by a Texas grand jury in September on a felony charge after injuring his then-4-year-old son by whipping him with a “switch,” a tree branch.

Peterson, placed on the Commissioner’s Exempt List until his court case was settled, appealed the suspension based on his belief that an NFL official had promised him he would get credit for “time served,” plus a two-game suspension.

“I conclude that the player has not demonstrated that the process and procedures surrounding his discipline were not fair and consistent,” Henderson wrote in an eight-page letter explaining his decision. “I find no basis to vacate or reduce the discipline.”

The players’ union argued that Peterson was being retroactively punished by the league’s new “enhanced discipline” personal conduct policy as it related to domestic violence. That policy was introduced in August — three months after Peterson whipped his son.

The union also pointed out in Monday’s court filing, as the Peterson camp has done for some time, that NFL Executive Vice President for Football Operations Troy Vincent said in a telephone conversation on Nov. 12 with the running back that the Viking would only be punished with “time served” on the Exempt List plus a two-game suspension. The two talked six days before Peterson’s no-contest plea.

However, the court petition read, “the commissioner ultimately decided to punish Mr. Peterson more harshly because he … declined to attend a pre-disciplinary hearing with NFL advisers on domestic violence, which is not provided for in the [collective bargaining agreement].”

Earlier Monday, ABC News aired what it said was audio from that telephone conversation.
