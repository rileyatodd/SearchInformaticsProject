Lone madman or a networked holy warrior?

The Drum
By John Blaxland

Updated December 16, 2014 09:25:58

Photo: The idea of Man Haron Monis' actions representing a broader threat to society is overstated. (ABC News)

Following the Sydney siege, we must avoid defaulting to the view that any criminal act that includes a garbled interpretation of the Islamic message is part of a planned, pre-meditated and co-ordinated attack, writes John Blaxland.

The hostage incident in Sydney's Martin Place has been resolved with the captor, a recent convert from Shia to Sunni Islam with a criminal record killed, along with two hostages and several others injured.

Yet this bizarre incident and the strange circumstances raises more questions than it answers.

Is this a symptom of an emerging civilizational "War Amongst the People" or the act of just another unhinged lone madman?

Sympathy notes from Canada's PM Stephen Harper and India's Narendra Modi point to the echoes of terrorist incidents at Mumbai's Taj Mahal Hotel in 2008 and the recent shootings at Ottawa's War Memorial and Parliament House. But how much caution should we exercise in responding on this premise?

There is a growing popular inclination to see this as part of a global initiative - an existential threat to the world under siege from fundamentalist Islamist extremism.

There are also those disposed to react against the otherness of Islam and the extremism this incident appears to demonstrate. The motivations of the captor appear to reflect a garbled and fringe interpretation of the Islamic message.

Stories of other threatening signs at other Sydney landmarks reinforce the fears and perceptions that this was a planned, pre-mediated and co-ordinated act.

Yet it is vitally important to avoid simply defaulting to this view. Now is not the time for shrill or emotive reactions.

Instead a cool, careful and collected response is required to prevent this incident further polarising, alienating or inciting long-established but sometimes marginalised elements of our multicultural community.

Yet as we seek to connect the dots, with international parallels and other domestic incidents pointing to trouble brewing, it seem clear we have a problem. But just how big is it?

On its own, a hostage incident like this would be discounted as the work of a madman. That view would be confirmed were there to be, say, a fascist banner or the demands of a resentful estranged parent rather than a Muslim banner on display in the shop window. It would be seen as a relatively insignificant crime that may or may not have made headlines beyond Australian shores.

It seems clear however that, like the lone and unhinged zealot in Ottawa who was a recent convert to Islam, this criminal has acted with the media spotlight in mind, selecting a shop across the road from television studios in Martin Place.

The banner displayed carried a universal Muslim message - The Shahada - reiterating the belief in Allah as the one true God and Mohammad as his messenger. The banner was not the one now most commonly associated with the so-called Islamic State. But the incident echoes the sentiment expressed by its leader Al Baghdadi, who has called for such acts of violence against non-believers.

Indeed, the Islamic edge to this incident feeds the growing concern about further prospects of violent jihadist extremism.

Perhaps the most significant message to emerge as the incident unfolded was the one published by the Australian National Imam's Council, which "unequivocally" condemned this criminal act, declaring that "such actions are denounced in part and in whole in Islam".

This heartening message demonstrates a resolve to discredit the incident as unworthy of any endorsement as a pious act that could possibly warrant even tacit support.

To be sure, there are parts of the Koran that refer to unspeakable acts of barbarity against non-Muslims. But there are also many parts of the holy texts of other religions that are downright brutal as well - not the least of which is the Old Testament in the Bible.

For the overwhelming majority of adherents of major world religions those harsh texts are hardly central tenets of faith. Thankfully Jesus's teachings in the New Testament stressed the virtues of loving ones neighbour and turning the other cheek.

Even though the captor claimed his actions were linked to the conflict in Iraq that does not prove that Australia's military actions there triggered it. The perpetrator already had a track record of unrelated violence. Conversely, it also seems to demonstrate that no matter how effective, precise and capable our soldiers, sailors and airmen may be in performing their duties in Iraq, defeating the so-called Islamic State in Iraq can do little to address the internal security challenges faced within Australia.

The argument that operations there make us safer here ring hollow.

At the same time, the scale of the incident was an existential one, but only for those directly facing the barrel of the captor's weapon. The idea of this being symptomatic of a broader existential threat to society is overstated.

Yet it is important for us all as Australians to respond graciously, respectfully and lawfully, trusting that the institutions of state, the police and security authorities, are the ones to manage aberrant behaviour.

As we do so, we should bear in mind John Dickson's wise exhortation on The Drum a few weeks back to reach out a hand of friendship to strangers who may be feeling uneasy, threatened or marginalised.

The Prime Minister spoke of Australia as a peaceful, open and generous society. He's right on that point. But the future of our peaceful multicultural nation cannot be taken for granted. The captor's message and his agenda must be countered by us all rejecting the messages of hate, fear, disrespect and of violence.
