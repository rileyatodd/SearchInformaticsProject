How to honour the victims of the Sydney siege

The Drum
By Tim Mayfield

Updated December 16, 2014 08:30:10

Photo: As we consider this shocking, unsettling, and ultimately violent act, let us choose to respond calmly. (702 ABC Sydney: John Donegan)

After the Sydney siege, the most powerful response to this attack on our values and way of life does not lie with tactical assault groups, security agencies or even our politicians - it lies within all of us, writes Tim Mayfield.

The unimaginable yet inevitable has occurred with a high profile terrorist attack occurring in Australia.

Three people are dead and four injured as a result of the insane actions of Iranian cleric Man Haron Monis at the Lindt Chocolat Cafe in Martin Place in Sydney.

The reality is that even though our national leaders have warned us that this moment would arrive, nothing can fully cushion the blow to our national psyche.

It is now up to each and every one of us to take responsibility for ensuring that this incident does not become a catalyst for panic and overreaction or an excuse to turn on each other.

There is already evidence that, far from dividing us, this attack is bringing us together in a rare and unique way. More so than any national sporting victory or high ranking in a quality of living index, the events of the last 24 hours have the potential to reinforce all that is good about our society.

While our political leaders cannot prepare us psychologically for a terrorist act such as this, they can and have led the way in terms of the quality of their response. NSW Premier Mike Baird and NSW Police Commissioner Andrew Scipione have demonstrated outstanding leadership as the siege unfolded and culminated in the early hours of this morning.

There will inevitably be forensic examination of this event, including whether or not it could have been avoided. Regardless of the outcome of this post-mortem, the reality is that our governments and their security branches cannot stop every madman with a weapon, flag and hostile intent.

It can pre-empt, it can mitigate and it can respond but it cannot always prevent in this age of the lone-wolf operator.

Likewise, the Government cannot dictate our collective response to this assault on our common humanity.

So as we consider this shocking, unsettling, and ultimately violent act, let us choose to respond calmly, with confidence and with peace in our hearts.

This does not mean that we shouldn't feel anxious or angry or fearful. Rather, we can choose how to externalise these feelings - replacing anxiety with openness, fear with tolerance and anger with mateship.

In particular, let all of us in word and deed ensure that Australia's Islamic community knows that they are an important and welcome component of our national identity.

This is already occurring through the viral Twitter hashtag #illridewithyou in which people have been offering to accompany anyone feeling unsafe in public as a result of their Islamic attire.

It is a good start but it will take more than Twitter to reassure Australia's Islamic community that we are united with them.

The most powerful response to this attack on our values and way of life does not lie with tactical assault groups, security agencies or even our politicians. Rather, it lies within all of us.

Let all of us, in our own way, explain to Australia's Muslims that we know that this not their fault and nor is their religion the reason for the violence perpetrated at the Lindt Caf√©. Indeed, extremist Islam and the violence carried out in its name are symptomatic of larger, seismic forces. These forces - present throughout the Islamic world and especially the Middle East - include political repression, emotional and physical dislocation, economic malaise and seemingly endless conflict - echoing across generations and in all corners of the globe, including Australia.

This is not to be an apologist for the terrorists or the beliefs that they use to justify their abhorrent tactics.

Rather, we must understand and acknowledge that the Islamic community is as rich and diverse as any other religious grouping in this country. Furthermore, the twisted and distorted interpretation of this proud faith practised by men such as Man Haron Monis is but a vehicle for their pre-formed millenarian designs.

In nearly all instances, the marginalisation and hatred came first with the bastardised, quasi-religious overlay a mere justification for their violent intent.

The most powerful response to this attack on our values and way of life does not lie with tactical assault groups, security agencies or even our politicians. Rather, it lies within all of us.

As we mourn for the victims of this attack and their families, let us ensure that December 15 comes to be known as the day that Australia was changed for the better. The day that we chose to demonstrate to the world, and especially those that would seek to do us harm, that we are a confident, positive, outward looking people who refuse to be turned against each other in the face of adversity.

This is the best way to honour the victims of the Sydney siege.

Tim Mayfield is a freelance writer who has previously worked at the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade and the Department of Defence. He holds a Bachelor of Arts and a Master of Middle Eastern and Central Asian Studies from the ANU. View his full profile here.
