Manus Is: a foreseeable death that shames Australia

The Drum
By Greg Barns

Posted December 12, 2014 13:53:31

Photo: An asylum seeker holds a picture of Reza Barati at Manus Island. (AAP: Eoin Blackwell)

A Senate report has reiterated that if you treat human beings in a way that is callous, cruel or oppressive, then violence follows. It is inexcusable that Reza Berati died, writes Greg Barns.

Senate Committees examining politically contentious issues often produce partisan reports which do not assist in the understanding of the issues at hand. The Senate Committee into the death of a detainee at the Manus Island immigration centre in February of this year is fortunately an exception to the rule.

While the Coalition MPs on the committee predictably dissented from the majority view adopted by Labor and the Greens, the evidence gathered by the committee supports the recommendations and findings it has made.

It is a pity that Immigration Minister Scott Morrison and the Abbott Government are simply wishing the Manus Island report away and playing politics over the tragic death of Iranian asylum seeker Reza Berati between February 16-18 this year.

Mr Morrison wants to blame the Papua New Guinea government and the former Rudd government, which cut a deal with Port Moresby to house asylum seekers at the Manus Island centre. Instead, Mr Morrison needs to remember that he and his department have a non-delegable duty of care to ensure the safety of those it detains, irrespective of the location of detention.

The 201-page report sets out clearly how the Manus Island centre had been mismanaged in the months leading up to the February riots, and it concludes that the prospect of violence was certainly foreseeable.

This conclusion appears reasonable given that even the Abbott Government's own investigator Robert Cornall found in his assessment, published in May this year, that delays, lack of information about the PNG resettlement process, and the anger of asylum seekers, frustrated at the prospect of not reaching Australia, all contributed to the violence that erupted in February and which resulted in Mr Berati's death.

Those of us who have visited prisons and detention centres over the years know one thing - if you treat human beings in a way that is callous, cruel or oppressive, then violence follows. Prison riots in Australia have occurred over the past few decades (most infamously Bathurst, Pentridge in Melbourne and Fremantle) because the authorities had allowed such conditions to develop.

There is little point in doing what the Coalition senators on the Manus Island Committee, Ian Macdonald and Linda Reynolds, are doing in their short dissenting report, and that is seek to paint the majority report as a political exercise. Violence occurred at Manus Island in February this year because the Australian government and its contractors mismanaged the place.

It needs to be remembered also that the Australian Government is the appropriate entity to compensate Mr Berati's family for the loss of his life. The Commonwealth recently conceded in the Supreme Court of Victoria, in a decision handed down last month called AS v Minister for Immigration and Border Protection, that it has a non-delegable duty to provide reasonable health care to detainees.

That duty of care extends to ensuring that if the Commonwealth reasonably foresees that violence is brewing, it has to take steps to ensure the safety of detainees. That duty of care is also non-delegable.

The Manus Island Senate report's other recommendations ought not to be controversial in a civilised liberal democratic society. Recommendation 5 of the report is particularly important, and yet the Abbott Government is baulking at its implementation.

Recommendation 5 is about ensuring that UN representatives, lawyers, the Human Rights Commission, and journalists have access to the Manus Island centre and be allowed to meet with centre staff and detainees.

We are talking about persons who are detained for extensive periods of time, who have committed no offence, and who in many cases are traumatised by their life experiences. That these people ought to be able to speak to lawyers, human rights officials, the media and UN representatives should be something that occurs as a matter of course. It is entirely consistent with Australia's international human rights obligations about treatment of people in detention.

But Senator McDonald and Senator Reynolds have opposed this recommendation, arguing that this would infringe on Papua New Guinea's sovereignty! This is a claim that cannot be taken seriously given that the only reason the Manus Island centre is open is because of Australia's insistence that it will not fulfil its international law obligations to assess and process claims for refugee status within Australia. Papua New Guinea is a pawn in Australian political games.

The Manus Island report is an important document, not only because it deals sensibly with the issue of responsibility for violence resulting in death and seeks to improve transparency at the centre, but because it focuses again on the sheer cruelty of Australia's treatment towards asylum seekers.

It is inexcusable that serious violence happens in detention centres at all.

Greg Barns is a spokesman for the Australian Lawyers Alliance and barrister. View his full profile here.
