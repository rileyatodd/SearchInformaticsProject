Researchers try to find out When Birds lost their teeth

Submitted by Luis Georg on Mon, 12/15/2014 - 15:01

Birds are superb creatures for research teams across the world. Many scientific studies on their genetics have revealed lot of interesting information about evolution of species on the planet. Researchers have put in efforts to find the reason why birds don’t have teeth; though, the phenomenon can boil down to genetics.

It seems that their lack of teeth comes down to evolution of their genetics. Presently, most of the birds have curved beaks and remarkable digestive systems due to which they don’t face much trouble breaking down food. The remarkable digestion has removed the need for teeth.

The University of California researchers are putting efforts to find out the alteration in the birds’ genome that caused them to drop teeth. Research team has found that around 150 years ago, birds did have teeth.

The bird fossil, archaopteryx found in Germany in 1861 and it is found from that birds came from toothed reptiles. Actually, the closest living relative to the bird is the alligator. Performing tests on modern birds’ DNA could provide more results.

Presently, researchers are looking for the six genes which are needed for tooth production. All six are required for the formation of enamel and dentin. Mutations that may have deactivated these six genes have been analyzed by scientists.

A mutation has been found recently and it was found from that the birds finally lost their capability to form teeth because of this mutation.

“The presence of several inactivating mutations that are shared by all 48 bird species suggests that the outer enamel covering of teeth was lost around 116 million years ago”, said Mark Springer, a professor of biology at the University of California and one of the lead researchers related to the study.
