Outrage over $100 fares amid Sydney siege prompts Uber to offer free rides

Date
December 15, 2014 - 2:59PM
  • 27 reading now
  • (29)

Ben Grubb

Deputy technology editor

View more articles from Ben Grubb

Follow Ben on Twitter Follow Ben on Google+ Email Ben

  • Tweet
  • submit to reddit

  • Print

One Uber user reported $100 being the minimum fare. Photo: Matthew Leung/Mashable

Taxi and private hire car app Uber will offer free rides out of Sydney's CBD on Monday following criticism for charging passengers more than four times the usual rate while a siege is underway at Martin Place and the city is in lockdown.

The increase was due to "surge pricing", which sees the cost to use the app rapidly increase when supply is limited. It's designed as an incentive to get more drivers on the road.

Some users reported a minimum of $100 being charged by the app as the siege unfolded. The increase in cost is based on a computer algorithm and not determined by staff.

Following outcry, the company said in a statement to Fairfax Media that it was "in the process of refunding rides".

Advertisement

The company has previously come under fire for surge pricing during natural disasters in the US and was in July forced to introduce caps to the scheme after an agreement with New York Attorney-General Eric Schneiderman.

It's unclear whether such caps exist in Australia. Uber did not comment on this in a statement.

The caps in the US came into effect after a number of snowstorms.

"We are all concerned with events in CBD. Fares have increased to encourage more drivers to come online & pick up passengers in the area," Uber Sydney said initially on its Twitter account at 1.35pm AEDST.

Following user anger, Uber later said that trips beginning in the CBD would now be free for riders and that refunds would be issued.

"Higher rates are still in place to encourage drivers to get into the CBD," it said at 2.15pm. This means the company will cover the higher costs.

Twitter users reacted with mixed emotions.

"Thank you for fixing this situation," said one.

"Stop digging an even bigger hole," said another.
