Three dead in Sydney siege: Gunman had 'infatuation with extremism' - Tony Abbott

Australian Prime Minister Tony Abbott has offered his sympathy to the families of the two hostages who died this morning in the Sydney cafe siege.

The identity of one of the victims has been confirmed. The 38-year-old woman killed in the siege was mother-of-two Katrina Dawson.

AAP has confirmed Ms Dawson, who worked in the city, died when heavily-armed police stormed the Lindt cafe in Martin Place early this morning, ending a 16-hour stand-off.

She was one of two hostages killed, along with the gunman, self-described Muslim cleric Man Haron Monis.

It would take a long time to work out what happened in the siege and why, Mr Abbott said.

What we do know is the perpetrator was well known to state and commonwealth authorities, he said.

"He had a long history of violent crime, infatuation with extremism and mental instability."

"Tragically there are people in our community ready to engage in politically motivated violence.

"We are ready to deal with these people with the full force of law."

Sydneysiders could be proud of their calm on what was a mentally testing day, Mr Abbott said.

Raid began after shots fired inside cafe

Police raided the Lindt Chocolat Cafe after they heard a number of gunshots from inside, said New South Wales state police Commissioner Andrew Scipione.

"They made the call because they believed that at that time, if they didn't enter, there would have been many more lives lost," he said.

Watch: Gunman dead in Sydney siege

The gunman was identified as Man Haron Monis, who once was prosecuted for sending offensive letters to families of Australian troops killed in Afghanistan.


Hostages run to safety during a cafe siege in Sydney. Photo / AP


A hostage runs to armed tactical response police officers for safety. Photo / AP

Scipione wouldn't say whether the two hostages who were killed - a 34-year-old man and a 38-year-old woman - were caught in crossfire, or shot by the gunman. Among the four wounded was a police officer shot in the face.

"Until we were involved in this emergency action, we believe that no one had been injured. That changed. We changed our tactic," he said, adding that there had been a total of 17 hostages taken in the cafe when the siege began.

The standoff ended when a loud bang was heard from the cafe and five people ran out. Shortly after, police swooped in, amid heavy gunfire, shouts and flashes. A police bomb disposal robot also was sent into the building, but no explosives were found.

Lindt chairman: 'We are devastated'

The group that owns Sydney's Lindt cafe has pledged support for the victims of a hostage-taker, saying it's profoundly saddened by the deaths and injuries suffered.

Lindt & Sprungli chairman Ernst Tanner said the loss of two innocent lives at its Martin Place cafe was a heavy weight to bear.

The company has pledged support for the families of the dead, as well as the wounded and other survivors.

"Lindt & Sprungli is profoundly saddened and deeply affected about the death of innocent people," Mr Tanner said in a statement .

"We are devastated by the loss of their lives and that several others were wounded and had to experience such trauma."


An armed tactical response police officer grabs a hostage as she runs to flee from the cafe . Photo / AP

NSW Premier: 'An attack we never thought we'd see'

NSW Premier Mike Baird told this morning's news conference: "I come before you with the heaviest of hearts."

"Unbelievably overnight we have lost some of our own in an attack we would never thought we would see here in our city," he said.

Mr Baird said the government's thoughts and prayers were with the innocent victims of an "horrendous vicious attack."

"I want to say to their family and friends that everyone in NSW stands beside you," he said.

"My thoughts also remain with those hostages who have been freed.

"They will be provided every support they need in coming days and weeks and months."

An injured hostage is carried to an ambulance. Photo / AP

Gunman identified by police

Haron Monis was well known to police. Photo / AAP

The dramatic scene unfolded shortly after the gunman was identified by local media as Iranian-born Man Haron Monis, who is facing charges including sexual assault and accessory to murder in separate cases.

Watch: Sydney cafe hostage situation over

Monis has long been on officials' radar. Last year, he was sentenced to 300 hours of community service for writing offensive letters to families of soldiers killed in Afghanistan. He was later charged with being an accessory to the murder of his ex-wife. Earlier this year, he was charged with the sexual assault of a woman in 2002. He has been out on bail on the charges.

"This is a one-off random individual. It's not a concerted terrorism event or act. It's a damaged goods individual who's done something outrageous," his former lawyer, Manny Conditsis, told Australian Broadcasting Corp.

"His ideology is just so strong and so powerful that it clouds his vision for common sense and objectiveness," Conditsis said.

Police put body armour on ambulance personnel. Photo / AAP

Hostages forced to make video

A video showing four hostages being forced to make demands on behalf of Man Haron Monis, has emerged.

The hostages made three demands: For an Islamic State flag to be delivered in return for one hostage to be released, for the media to acknowledge the attack was one on Australia by Isis and two hostages would be released.

The third was for Tony Abbot to have a conversation with Monis that would be broadcast live in return for five hostages to be released.

The hostages sounded nervous as they delivered the messages and appeared to be reading from a script.

Watch the hostages' video here.

Link to Islamic State

Throughout the day, several people were seen with their arms in the air and hands pressed against the window of the cafe, and two people holding up a black flag with the Shahada, or Islamic declaration of faith, written on it.

The Shahada translates as "There is no god but God and Muhammad is his messenger." It is considered the first of Islam's five pillars of faith, and is similar to the Lord's Prayer in Christianity. It is pervasive throughout Islamic culture, including the green flag of Saudi Arabia. Jihadis have used the Shahada in their own black flag.

A number of Australian Muslim groups condemned the hostage-taking in a joint statement and said the flag's inscription was a "testimony of faith that has been misappropriated by misguided individuals."

An injured hostage is taken to a waiting ambulance. Photo / AP

In a show of solidarity, many Australians offered on Twitter to accompany people dressed in Muslim clothes who were afraid of a backlash from the cafe siege. The hashtag #IllRideWithYou was used more than 90,000 times by late last night.

Standoff continued for hours

Seven Network television news staff watched the gunman and hostages for hours from a fourth floor window of their Sydney offices, opposite the cafe.

The gunman could be seen pacing back and forth past the cafe's windows. Reporter Chris Reason said the man carried what appeared to be a pump-action shotgun, was unshaven and wore a white shirt and a black cap.

Earlier yesterday, network staff counted about 15 different faces among hostages forced up against the windows.

A blood soaked stretcher is wheeled to an ambulance early this morning. Photo / AP

"The gunman seems to be sort of rotating these people through these positions on the windows with their hands and faces up against the glass," Reason said in a report from the vantage point. "One woman we've counted was there for at least two hours -- an extraordinary, agonizing time for her surely having to stand on her feet for that long."

"When we saw that rush of escapees, we could see from up here in this vantage point the gunman got extremely agitated as he realized those five had got out. He started screaming orders at the people, the hostages who remain behind," he added.

Reason later reported that staff brought food from a kitchen at the rear of the cafe and the hostages were fed.

As night set in, the lights inside the cafe were switched off. Armed police guarding the area outside fitted their helmets with green-glowing night goggles.

St. Vincent's hospital spokesman David Faktor said a male hostage was in satisfactory condition in the hospital's emergency department. He was the only one of the freed hostages to be taken to a hospital, and Scipione said he was being treated for a pre-existing condition.

Hundreds of police blanketed the city as streets were closed and offices evacuated. The public was told to stay away from Martin Place, site of the state premier's office, the Reserve Bank of Australia, and the headquarters of two of the nation's largest banks. The state parliament house is a few blocks away, and the landmark Sydney Opera House also is nearby.


Hostages run towards armed tactical responce police as they run to freedom from the cafe. Photo / AP

"This is a very disturbing incident," Prime Minister Tony Abbott said. "It is profoundly shocking that innocent people should be held hostage by an armed person claiming political motivation."

Lindt Australia posted a message on its Facebook page thanking the public for its support.

"We are deeply concerned over this serious incident and our thoughts and prayers are with the staff and customers involved and all their friends and families," the company wrote.

PM John Key: NZ police well-equipped to cope with similar attack

John Key says if a "lone wolf" attack similar to the deadly Sydney siege happened in New Zealand, he was confident local police would deal with the situation effectively.

Mr Key called the Sydney siege a "terrible tragedy" and offered his support to all Australians today.

"They're our greatest mates," Mr Key told TV3's Firstline this morning.

Mr Key said it was disingenuous to suggest the Sydney siege was a direct consequence of Australia's military campaign against Islamic State (Isis) terrorists in the Middle East.

He told Firstline Isis was a highly-resourced terrorist group that targeted disenchanted or deluded people to create "domestic terror threats" in countries around the world.

"You've got some very sick people and you've got an organisation like Isis which is trying to target those very sick people."

The Prime Minister told TV3 it was difficult for authorities to stop every possible attack, even when an attacker was known to police.

He said there was a very high threshold for such people to be arrested and detained solely on the basis of suspicions they might have terrorist aspirations.

But he said New Zealand police were extremely well-trained and included specialist units who could combat hostage-takers.

Auckland-based security expert Dr Paul G. Buchanan said the Sydney siege was a "cautionary tale" that demonstrated even stringent anti-terrorist laws simply couldn't prevent such attacks.

Dr Buchanan told RadioLive the Sydney siege gunman was "unhinged" but not necessarily ideological, even though he may have had "political or religious" overtones to his worldview.

Dr Buchanan praised New South Wales police for not inflaming anti-Islamic sentiment or jumping on the bandwagon of labelling the siege a typical terrorist attack.

He said it was "impossible to prevent, much less predict" such a lone wolf attack.

New Zealand's Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Mfat) said it was unlikely any New Zealanders were among the people held hostage.

"The nationalities of hostages is still being worked through but there are no reports New Zealanders were involved," an Mfat spokeswoman said shortly before 8am.

The attack Australia was waiting for?


A policeman (L) and a paramedic (R) escort a hostage (C) away from the scene. Photo / AFP

Australia's government raised the country's terror warning level in September in response to the domestic threat posed by supporters of the Islamic State group. Counterterror law enforcement teams later conducted dozens of raids and made several arrests in Australia's three largest cities -- Melbourne, Sydney and Brisbane. One man arrested during a series of raids in Sydney was charged with conspiring with an Islamic State leader in Syria to behead a random person in downtown Sydney.

The Islamic State group, which now holds a third of Syria and Iraq, has threatened Australia in the past. In September, Islamic State group spokesman Abu Mohammed al-Adnani issued an audio message urging so-called "lone wolf" attacks abroad, specifically mentioning Australia. Al-Adnani told Muslims to kill all "disbelievers," whether they be civilians or soldiers.

One terrorism expert said the situation appeared to be that of a "lone wolf" making his own demands, rather than an attack orchestrated by a foreign jihadist group.

"There haven't been statements from overseas linking this to extremist groups outside the country -- that is quite positive," said Charles Knight, lecturer in the Department of Policing, Intelligence and Counter Terrorism at Australia's Macquarie University. "The individual or individuals involved didn't kill early, which is part of the pattern of some recent international attacks. ... It seems to be shifting more into the model of a traditional hostage situation, rather than the sort of brutal attacks we've seen overseas."
