Sony fights hack damage as new threats emerge

Elizabeth Weise and Andrea Mandell, USA TODAY 5:23 p.m. EST December 15, 2014

A screen grab of a message purporting to be from the Guardians of Peace, a group that claims it is behind the massive hack on Sony Pictures Entertainment.(Photo: Elizabeth Weise)

LOS ANGLES Beleaguered Sony Pictures Entertainment tried to stem the damage of the two-week-old hack attack on Monday even as new threats from the hackers emerged.

Sony Pictures Entertainment organized an all-hands meeting Monday afternoon to talk to staff about the ongoing aftermath of the massive hack that struck the company Nov. 24.

The move comes on the heels of a post that seems to indicate the hackers who call themselves the "Guardians of Peace" are planning to release a "Christmas gift" as they term it of "larger quantities of data" that will put Sony Pictures "into the worst state."

As the fallout from the hack continues, Sony has been trying to shut down press reports about what the hackers have posted so far. These have included embarrassing e-mails from highly placed studio staff, pay rates and, most recently, the full script to an upcoming James Bond movie.

There are other attempts to close off the spigot of stolen e-mails. The FBI tried to visit at least one security professional who had downloaded some of the stolen Sony files. These have been posted to public sharing sites over the past two weeks.

On Dec. 6, computer security specialist Dan Tentler tweeted that the FBI had visited his home in San Diego. In a tweet, he said, "Just to warn other security folk working on the Sony leaks the FBI just visited my home."

Tentler had been posting about what he'd found in the Sony files on his blog, and doing interviews about the material with media outlets, including USA TODAY.

He has not posted on the Sony hack since. He did not respond to e-mails on Monday.

Reached Monday, the FBI had no comment on the visit or whether it is illegal to write about stolen files that are publicly available.

FBI agents also met with Sony employees on Dec. 10 about the hack. A Sony employee who was not authorized to speak on behalf of the company said that agents gave them basic advice about changing passwords and how to protect themselves.

On Sunday, Sony Pictures, via prominent lawyer David Boies, sent a letter to media organizations warning against use of the stolen data.

There doesn't appear to be legal support for the warning, said Kurt Opsahl, deputy general counsel for the Electronic Frontier Foundation, an Internet free-speech non-profit based in San Francisco.

"Under Supreme Court precedent (Bartnicki v. Vopper), a media outlet cannot be held liable for publishing information illegally obtained by a third party, especially when the information is a matter of public concern," Opsahl said.

On Monday, screenwriter Aaron Sorkin penned a strongly worded defense of the company, targeted at media publishing accounts from Sony Co-Chairman Amy Pascal's hacked inbox.

The threats to release more material, purported to be from the Guardians of Peace, were made on Saturday and Sunday to Pastebin, a file-sharing site.

In the Saturday posting, someone who claimed to be one of the hackers wrote:

We are preparing for you a Christmas gift.

The gift will be larger quantities of data.

And it will be more interesting.

The gift will surely give you much more pleasure and put Sony Pictures into the worst state.

Please send an email titled by "Merry Christmas" at the addresses below to tell us what you want in our Christmas gift."

Below the posting were five e-mail addresses on YOPmail, a disposable e-mail address site.

The Pastebin posting was quickly taken down, but USA TODAY obtained a cached copy of it.

The Sunday posting's message was:

We are preparing for you a Christmas gift.

The gift will be larger quantities of data.

And it will be more interesting.

The gift will surely give you much more pleasure and put Sony Pictures into the worst state.

Please send an email titled by "Merry Christmas" at the addresses below to tell us what you want in our Christmas gift."

The posting went on to reiterate that Sony must acquiesce to the hackers' demands. Neither the hackers nor Sony Pictures Entertainment has stated publicly what those demands, if any, were.

The Sunday message said, "The sooner SPE accept our demands, the better, of course. The farther time goes by, the worse state SPE will be put into and we will have Sony go bankrupt in the end."

The message also told Sony staffers that the hackers planned to release "emails and privacy of the Sony Pictures employees." If they didn't want their information released, employees were instructed to "tell us your name and business title to take off your data."

No information was included about how Sony staffers were to do so.
