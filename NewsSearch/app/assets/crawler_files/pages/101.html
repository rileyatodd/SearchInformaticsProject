Is it time for the Fed to drop its 'considerable time' language on near-zero percent interest rate?
Fed officials must decide if they want to signal an interest rate hike amid growing financial market turmoil

The Federal Reserve could signal Wednesday that the time is approaching for the first interest rate hike in more than eight years -- and "time" is the word that investors and analysts will be searching for in the central bank's policy statement.

The policymaking Federal Open Market Committee will release its statement at 11 a.m. Pacific time.

Half an hour later, Fed Chairwoman Janet L. Yellen will hold a news conference discussing the statement as the Fed tries to navigate monetary policy amid growing financial market turmoil triggered by plunging oil prices.

Yellen and the other Fed policymakers have to decide if they want to potentially further roil markets by indicating that a rise in the central bank's benchmark short-term interest rate was coming soon.

The main question for the Fed is if the timing is right to remove its language that interest rates will remain low for a "considerable time."

In addition to concerns about weakening economic conditions abroad, particularly in Russia, Fed officials also are grappling with low inflation.

On Wednesday, the Labor Department reported that the Consumer Price Index declined 0.3% in November. That was the largest drop in six years, fueled by a steep fall in gasoline prices.

Overall, consumer prices rose just 1.3% in the 12 months ended Nov. 30, well below the Fed's 2% annual inflation target.

The Fed might not want to risk deflation by increasing interest rates too soon. So Fed officials must decide if lower oil prices are just a temporary drag on inflation.

So-called core inflation, which excludes volatile energy and food prices, was up 1.7% in the year ended Nov. 30.

"The FOMC is likely to make the case in its policy statement ... that the dampening impact of lower energy prices on overall inflation will be only transitory," said Stuart Hoffman, chief economist at PNC Financial Services.

"But even if lower energy prices are not pushing down core inflation, they are certainly keeping it steady," he said.

With the U.S. economy, particularly the jobs market, continuing to improve, Fed watchers anticipated that the central bank could begin to lay the groundwork for an interest rate hike next year by ditching the reference to "considerable time" on Wednesday.

"My sense is they’ll drop that and adopt something more neutral," said Mark Zandi, chief economist at Moody's Analytics.

In September 2012, the Fed began trying to reassure financial markets by saying it expected to maintain a “highly accommodative” monetary policy -- a near-zero percent short-term interest rate -- “for a considerable time after the economic recovery strengthens.”

The term "considerable time" has been in Fed policy statements ever since.

In December 2012, the Fed said that the policy would last for “a considerable time” after its bond-buying stimulus program ended.

This week's two-day meeting of the open market committee is the first since it ended the bond buying in October.

A change in language Wednesday makes sense because Yellen would be able to explain it during her quarterly news conference. Her next news conference isn't until March.

The last Fed rate hike was in June 2006, to 5.25%. The following year, as the economy began tumbling toward the Great Recession, the Fed began slowly lowering rates.

By December 2008, the Fed dropped the rate to zero percent and it has stayed there as the economy struggled through the recession and the slow recovery.

Zandi said the Fed could start indicating it would be "patient" in deciding when to start raising interest rates.

That's the word the Fed used in 2004 when it laid the groundwork for the first interest rate hike in four years.

In August 2003, the Fed first used the term "considerable period" to describe how long rates would remain low.

In January 2004, five months before it raised rates, the Fed changed its language to say it would "be patient in removing its policy accommodation.”

Fed policymakers kept the "patient" language until May 2004, when they said they believed "that policy accommodation can be removed at a pace that is likely to be measured."

A month later, the Fed raised its 1% interest rate by 0.25 percentage points. It then raised the rate in the same increment at each of its final five meetings of the year.

By the end of 2004, the rate was 2.25%.
