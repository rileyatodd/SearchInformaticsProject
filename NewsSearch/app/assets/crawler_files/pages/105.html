Fed

Live Blog: Fed Decision and Janet Yellen’s Press Conference

  • Article
  • Comments

  • central banking

  • Federal Open Market Committee

  • Interest Rates

  • Janet Yellen

  • Live Blogging now
Janet Yellen, chairwoman of the U.S. Federal Reserve, speaks in September.
Bloomberg News

The Federal Reserve wraps up its final policy meeting of the year Wednesday with a big decision: how to update its guidance about when interest rates might start rising.

The statement from the Federal Open Market Committee is due at 2 p.m., followed by Fed Chairwoman Janet Yellen’s 2:30 press conference. We’ll be following all the action live here throughout the afternoon.

    • 1:28 pm
    • A Complication for the Fed: Inflation May Be Heading Much Lower
    • by Josh Zumbrun
    • Edit
    • Add a Comment

    The official inflation statistics are currently far behind the readings produced by real-time measures of inflation. That’s mostly because of how rapidly prices are dropping due to the oil price collapse.

    The Fed, for example, closely follows the personal consumption expenditures price index, but the most recent data available for that index are from October.

    One real-time measure of inflation, commonly known as the Billion Prices Project, scrapes prices from the internet every day to track movements. The index is formally known as the State Street’s PriceStats Daily U.S. Inflation Index and it’s showing a dramatic slowdown through mid-December that’s not yet reflected in the official measures.

    Once the official statistics catch up, this could be an inflation picture that looks pretty ugly to the Fed.

     

    • 1:26 pm
    • Does the Fed Still View Falling Oil Prices as a Positive?
    • by Josh Zumbrun
    • Edit
    • Add a Comment

    One thing we’ll be closely watching for in the Fed statement and Ms. Yellen’s press conference: any shifts in their view about the plunge in oil prices.

    We discussed this in more detail in Monday’s Outlook column. 

    In short : falling oil prices should give a boost to consumers, possibly even helping the Fed get to its full employment goal a little sooner. But the prices will send inflation downward, putting the central bank’s 2% inflation goal even further out of reach.

    It looks like they’re placing more weight on the boost to consumers and aren’t getting too worried about what might happen to inflation. We’ll be watching for any hints that this outlook is shifting.

     

    • 1:25 pm
    • Non-Cuba News in the World Today
    • by Sudeep Reddy
    • Edit
    • Add a Comment

    The Fed is facing three big global developments it will need to assess and explain in the news conference:

     

    • Oil. The price of crude oil has plunged more than 40% since mid-June, despite today’s pop in the market, due to a combination of supply and demand factors. What that means for growth vs. inflation will be critical to the Fed’s outlook. (More on that here shortly.)
    • Russia. The turmoil in Russia, due to both sanctions and the oil plunge, has been hitting emerging markets hard for weeks. How the Fed looks at the impact of its policies on other currencies and emerging markets (including financial stability) has central bankers all over the world watching today very closely.
    • Global growth. It’s slow and slowing. How long can the U.S. strengthen in a sea of trouble?

    If you’ve been here before, you know the drill very well. The Federal Open Market Committee’s statement comes at 2 p.m., along with new economic projections from all Fed officials. Then we’ll see Janet Yellen at 2:30 for her press conference.

    If you’re keeping score at home, this will be the Fed’s 16th press conference since Ben Bernanke started the tradition in 2011. Yellen was responsible for creating the format, so she owns it now. It will be her fourth press conference since she took the baton in January.

    We’ve followed all 15 previous press conferences here on Real Time Economics. So feel free to take a trip through the archives back when we were talking about the end of a bond-buying program, turbulence abroad and the challenges to Fed communications.     • 12:45 pm
    • A Considerable Fed Meeting
    • by Sudeep Reddy
    • Edit
    • Add a Comment

    Welcome to another big day in Fed land. America’s central bankers have been meeting for two days to discuss to death two words in the Federal Reserve’s policy statement: “considerable time.”

    That’s how long the Fed had said it would wait to raise interest rates after its bond-buying program ended. Well, the bond-buying ended after the Fed’s last meeting in October so now it needs new words.

    With the U.S. economy gaining steam and the global economy losing steam, losing “considerable time” will be only part of the story today. We’ll get the Fed’s updated assessment of the economic environment and how it might navigate a considerably turbulent environment in the coming year.
