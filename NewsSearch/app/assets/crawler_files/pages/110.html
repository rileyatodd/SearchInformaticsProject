BlackBerry Ltd. Classic review: A device that takes users back to the future

Lynn Greiner, Special to Financial Post | December 17, 2014 | Last Updated: Dec 17 12:43 PM ET

BlackBerry Ltd. has a lot at stake Wednesday. After its successful Passport launch, aimed at users craving cutting-edge technology, it is trying to lure the corporate users whose old Bolds and Torches would have to be pried from their cold, dead hands into the modern age of BB10, without destroying the user experience they know and love (or at least, are familiar with).

Enter the BlackBerry Classic.

BlackBerry Classic: Key Specs

• BlackBerry keyboard, trackpad and classic navigation keys

• BlackBerry 10 web browser is “astoundingly beautiful” and three times faster than that on the Bold 9900, company says

• Display: 3.5-inch touchscreen display, 294 dpi HD resolution and Corning Gorilla Glass 3

• Battery: Up to 22 hours of battery life

• Components: 1.5 GHz Qualcomm Snapdragon Processor, 2GB RAM, 16GB of device storage (expandable by up to another 128GB via Micro SD card)

• Camera: 2MP front- and 8MP rear-facing camera with enhanced optics and upgraded imaging sensors

• Chen says the Classic may come out in different colours later, including bronze, blue

At just under ¾-inch taller than the Bold 9900, and a smidge (6mm, if you’re feeling picky) wider, the Classic looks and feels like a traditional BlackBerry, with a few modern twists. Its 3.5-inch, 720 x 720 screen is clear and bright; it’s pretty much the same resolution as the Bold (though it can’t hold a candle to the Passport). That extra height over the Bold is all screen, which makes the display much more readable. I found it quite comfortable to use, even when browsing the web.

The big twist: the display is a capacitive multi-touch touchscreen — and, for durability, it’s Gorilla Glass.

The primary 8 mp camera (the same resolution as the one in the iPhone 6, for what it’s worth) does a respectable job; though again, it doesn’t match the Passport’s 13 mp shooter. But the Classic isn’t for the Passport’s audience, it’s meant for the crowd for whom 8 mp is a whopping improvement. The front camera is a fairly typical 2 mp.

The big change for a BB10 device user, however, is no change whatever for a Bold user: the “belt” is back. This row of controls above the keyboard — with its answer and end phone call buttons, menu and back buttons, and mini-trackpad — has been sorely missed by BB10 converts. Yes, you can perform all of those functions on the touchscreen, but some things — especially editing, copying, and pasting text — are more easily done with the trackpad’s cursor controls. As a former BlackBerry Bold user, I found myself quickly reverting to the trackpad in those circumstances. However, I also found myself bouncing between old and new methods of closing apps and calling up menus. Both work nicely.

The backlit QWERTY keyboard is most definitely a BlackBerry keyboard. I found myself thumb typing at speed within minutes. Users of the older handsets won’t have any trouble adapting.

There are a few small downsides to the Classic. For one thing, the battery is not removable. That’s not a huge issue, because in the admittedly short time I’ve had the device, battery life has been excellent. It went an entire weekend on a charge, and still had juice left. That wasn’t a fluke, either – it is still going strong after a recharge and a couple of busy workdays.

That battery life comes with a price. The Classic tips the scales at 176 g (just over 6 oz). It’s well-balanced, so it doesn’t feel that heavy, but it’s a good couple of ounces heavier than the Bold. This is not one of your razor-thin, super-light devices.

Read our coverage of the Classic launch as it happened

The Classic comes with a nice collection of sensors: Accelerometer, Magnetometer, Gyroscope, Proximity, Hall Sensor, and Ambient Light Sensor. It also has a built-in GPS, integrated with the Maps app. Connectivity-wise, aside from its cellular functionality, it supports 802.11 a/b/g/n WiFi, and can function as a mobile hotspot. You also get Bluetooth 4.0 and, although the official specs we received don’t mention it, NFC — making mobile payments possible.

The internal 16 GB storage may sound a bit small, but it’s augmented by a micro SD slot that supports hot swapping of cards, providing virtually unlimited capacity.

The Classic comes loaded with BlackBerry 10.3.1 OS. There are differences from the Bold’s BB OS 7 (often good ones, like the Hub), which will require some adaptation, but BlackBerry has restored familiar keyboard shortcuts to the OS that had been previously removed from the 10.x OS. It’s a welcome change. For example, users can again type C to compose a new email, and can start typing a name on the Home screen to pull up a contact to call or email, saving several steps over the initial version on BB10 OS. The new BlackBerry Assistant vastly improves and extends the old voice controls. There will be a learning curve, but it shouldn’t be too onerous.

Camera users will enjoy the enhanced apps, which include the ability to edit pictures and a Time Shift function that rapidly grabs multiple versions of an image so you can create a perfect shot in which everyone’s eyes are open.

If BB10 apps aren’t sufficient, BlackBerry has included the ability to install Android apps from the Amazon app store. The Amazon store seems strong on play, but light on business apps — I hunted in vain for the Android app for Marriott hotels, which is on Google Play, for example.

All in all, however, I’m happy with the Classic. The hardware is solid, albeit a tad heavy. The keyboard is pure BlackBerry, a good thing indeed. BlackBerry has done a good job tweaking the operating system to accommodate old and new; the browser in particular has been brought into this century.

I suspect that anyone who loved their old BlackBerry Bold (or anyone who wants a phone with a good keyboard) will love the Classic. And that, I’m told, was the plan.

It worked.

The BlackBerry Classic will be available for $499 with no contract (and directly from ShopBlackBerry.com), and for $49 with a two year contract from Bell or Rogers. Telus charges $50 for the device, with a two year contract. Wind Mobile has also announced it will carry the Classic, though it hasn’t publicized pricing yet.
