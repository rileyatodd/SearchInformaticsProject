Former employees file class-action lawsuits against Sony over hacking scandal

Two former Sony employees filed a suit on behalf of themselves and around 15,000 other workers claiming Sony knew its network systems were vulnerable, but made a 'business decision' not to fix them. A second lawsuit filed by two production managers faulted the studio's decision making in regards to 'The Interview.'

BY Philip Caulfield , Kirthana Ramisetti
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
Published: Tuesday, December 16, 2014, 2:44 PM
Updated: Tuesday, December 16, 2014, 6:40 PM

Two former Sony employees slapped the company with a lawsuit for allegedly failing to protect their private information from hackers.

Lawyers for ex-Sony workers Michael Corona and Christina Mathis claimed in the suit filed in Los Angeles federal court Monday that Sony knew its computer systems were vulnerable, but made a "business decision" not to patch any holes. 

"An epic nightmare, much better suited to a cinematic thriller than to real life, is unfolding in slow motion for Sony's current and former employees," the pair's legal team said in the complaint, which was posted on HollywoodReporter.com.

"Their most sensitive data, including over 47,000 Social Security numbers, employment files including salaries, medical information, and anything else that their employer Sony touched, has been leaked to the public, and may even be in the hands of criminals."

Corona worked for Sony Pictures Entertainment from 2004 to 2007, while Mathis worked for Sony Pictures Consumer Products from 2000 and 2002, the suit said.

The pair filed the suit on behalf of themselves and around 15,000 other current and former employees.

The complaint slammed the corporation for "inexcusable problems," alleging that, although struck by hackers before, Sony "failed to secure its computer systems, servers, and databases, despite weaknesses that it has known about for years."

Sony also "failed to timely protect confidential information of its current and former employees from law-breaking hackers," the suit said.

"Put simply, Sony knew about the risks it took with its past and current employees' data," the suit said. "Sony gambled, and its employees — past and current — lost."

Corona, of Virginia, said in the suit hackers gained access to his full name, Social Security Number, birthday, former address, salary history other information, while Mathis, of California, said her social and former address were stolen.

Both said they'd already spent dozens of hours and shelled out hundreds of dollars on identity theft protection and other measures.

Mathis also claimed the company had been largely mum about the case, sending her a form letter when she emailed about the breach.

So far, Sony hasn't commented on the suit, which was filed late Monday by the Seattle-based firm Keller Rohrback.

It was seeking unspecified damages.

A second class-action lawsuit was filed Tuesday by lead plaintiffs Susan Dakow and Yvonne Yaconelli, production managers on films such as "Spider-Man" and "Jerry Maguire," according to The Hollywood Reporter.

As in the first lawsuit, the new suit slams the studio for not taking better security precautions prior to the hack perpetrated by Guardians of Peace.

But they also have a second claim against the studio in regards to "The Interview," which is thought to be the motivation for the cyber attack against the studio.

"Various news reports suggest the original script of 'The Interview' introduced a fake villain, but that Sony specifically changed the script to make North Korea's leader Kim Jong Un the film's villain," the lawsuit stated.

"Upon information and belief, Sony knew it was reasonably forseeable that producing a script about North Korea's leader Kim Jong Un would cause a backlash."

The lawsuit would aim to explore whether the Seth Rogen-James Franco comedy "created an unreasonable risk that Plantiffs' and Class Members' PII (personally identifiable information) would be exposed."

News of the second lawsuit comes after hackers issued threats against the New York City premiere of "The Interview," which is scheduled for Thursday, as well as upcoming screenings of the film, which is set to open Christmas Day. Rogen and Franco have canceled all promotional appearances for their film in the wake of the hackers' warnings.
