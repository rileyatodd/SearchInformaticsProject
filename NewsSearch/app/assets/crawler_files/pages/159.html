                                      Oliver Luck could be valuable bridge to NCAA's future

                                      December 17, 2014 12:32 PM ET

                                      West Virginia athletic director Oliver Luck's move to the No. 2 position at the NCAA office is noteworthy for three reasons.

                                      First, Luck will have to leave the College Football Playoff Selection Committee as a Big 12 representative. It's doubtful he will shed tears on that while living in the same city as his son, Indianapolis Colts star Andrew Luck. But hey, at least Art Briles can now search for a Texan to join the ensemble so no future committee member gets buried in Maryland.

                                      Second, Luck's role overseeing the day-to-day operations in all NCAA regulatory functions could signify a greater role for athletic directors. This is something athletic directors have been wanting. They've got it in the new NCAA governance structure and now with Luck's new role. Athletic directors are officially on the clock to help tackle big issues.

                                      Third, and most interestingly, Luck comes to the NCAA as a forward thinker and one of the most pragmatic ADs about the legal challenges facing college sports. Is Luck going to be assimilated into the NCAA rhetoric about defending amateurism as court cases and appeals continue? Or does his hiring represent progress by the NCAA and a wink-and-nod acknowledgment that allowing players to be paid is coming to collegiate athletics?

                                      “This is a time of fundamental change in intercollegiate athletics that will set the foundation for the years ahead,” Luck said in an NCAA statement. “The challenges both internal and external to the NCAA present a unique opportunity to help shape the landscape for hundreds of thousands of young men and women.”

                                      Back in October 2012, Luck was ahead of his time by publicly expressing concern that no plan was in place for college sports' future if the NCAA lost the Ed O'Bannon case, which focused on the commercialized use of college athletes' names, images and likenesses. Luck headed up a committee within the Big 12 to explore those issues.

                                      “We'll have to create what I imagine is some kind of national system to accomplish what all the schools and the NCAA have tried to do to promote events and generate revenue,” Luck told AL.com in 2012. “What's the proper balance? That really is the key issue. It doesn't seem like there's much discussion on this.”

                                      Indeed there was not, at least not publicly. Luck's voice got silenced for a little while after those comments and leading into the O'Bannon trial last June. He reemerged during a Big 12 forum last August about the future of college sports on the eve of the NCAA losing the O'Bannon case.

                                      “I have a lot of respect for a person's name, image and likeness,” Luck said. “That is who you are. That is your identity. That's sort of existential, and ultimately I think the answer is if we are, in fact, using the name, image and likeness of the student athlete, and I don't think he or she waives that right just because they accept a scholarship, just like a drama major who might be in the school play doesn't waive his or her right in terms of using the name, image and likeness, I think they should be compensated for use of that name, image and likeness.

                                      “Now, in many cases that will be relatively little money. Every now and then there's a Johnny Manziel or a star player who does generate a good bit, but I do think it would be appropriate for universities to respect what I could consider a constitutional right, your name, image and likeness and figure out a way to compensate those student athletes for that.”

                                      Last week at the IMG Intercollegiate Athletics Forum, athletic directors went through the usual talking points about the prospect of allowing players to be paid, along with the common doom-and-gloom scenarios. Aside from Notre Dame athletic director Jack Swarbrick saying athletes should be paid through group licensing deals, the forum was a missed opportunity to talk ideas and move the conversation forward instead of clinging to fear.

                                      Because make no mistake, the conversation will have to pick up steam sooner rather than later. Change is coming, whether people want to accept it or not.

                                      Pending the results of the NCAA appeal from the O'Bannon ruling, college sports is less than nine months away from a federal judge's injunction allowing schools to offer deferred payments to FBS football players and Division I men's basketball players for use of their name, image and likeness. The new benefits would start in the 2016-17 academic year.

                                      The NCAA is allowed to cap those deferred payments at no less than $5,000 per year. NCAA president Mark Emmert said he believes creating a rule to address the injunction would require a vote from all of Division I, not just the Power Five conferences through autonomy.

                                      Good luck reaching a consensus on that. If Luck thought getting 12 CFP committee members to agree on four football teams was tough, try getting 351 Division I members to agree on a rule allowing players to be paid.

                                      But there's a pressing need to figure this out. If the NCAA can't determine how to cap the payment, the injunction sort of morphs into the Jeffrey Kessler case that's playing out in a courtroom as we speak. The O'Bannon injunction without a cap would mean a free market on what schools could promise to pay players when their college career is over.

                                      Luck has his hands full, but if his hiring means the NCAA is truly recognizing that change is coming, Luck could be a valuable bridge to whatever the future looks like.
