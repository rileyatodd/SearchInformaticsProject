    Curiosity Finds a Tantalizing Clue About Life on Mars

    While monitoring the Red Planet's levels of methane—a compound typically created by biological processes, at least on Earth—the Curiosity rover detects some mysterious and tantalizing spikes.

    By John Wenz
    Email
    Comments
    Share

    Mars

    NASA

    December 16, 2014 1:30 PM Text Size: A . A . A
    Something strange is afoot at Gale Crater.

    As the Curiosity rover drives around this region of Mars, it’s been measuring the levels of methane in the atmosphere. This is crucial data because those measurements could tell scientists something about whether Mars is, or ever was, inhabited—most methane on Earth comes from biological sources.

    So far, the rover has typically found levels a little lower that what scientists expected. But then, out of nowhere, Curiosity has seen the methane levels on Mars spike tenfold. So what is going on?

    The naturally occurring methane on Mars’ surface is the byproduct of organic material decay from meteorites and other dust. Curiosity typically detected about 0.7 part per billion, but over a two month period, the local methane levels spiked to 7 parts per billion. To NASA JPL senior research scientist Chris Webster, a co-author on a new study out today in Science, this indicates a localized methane source. They’re not ruling out the most tantalizing origin, either.

    “At this stage, we cannot rule out the possibility that microbial life is contributing today to those levels of methane or contributed in the past,” Webster said in a phone interview with Popular Mechanics.

    By past contributions, Webster means that the methane could have been produced by microbes in the past, trapped in the soil, and then released onto the surface. The fact that the methane levels don’t rise overall suggests that it comes in pockets rather than in one big spike.

    This finding sheds new light on a tantalizing finding from 2009. Back then, researchers reported huge, but localized, releases of methane on the Red Planet. But subsequent observations turned up no such methane, and the story quickly retreated into the background. In fact, the Curiosity team had written off those 2009 observations after the methane failed to turn up in the rover’s initial observations—for eight months, there was nothing but the smallest traces of the gas. But then, the huge spike appeared.

    “That was closure on the issue, until last November, when we were totally surprised to see the high level emerge,” Webster says. The new observations suggest that something is releasing localized bursts of methane.

    Curiosity can’t go hunt for the source of the methane, something Webster calls a “wild goose chase.” Instead, it will continue on its primary mission to Mount Sharp, though the rover could record some related data at Mount Sharp, such as ratios of carbon-13, an isotope that could indicate biological processes.

    The MAVEN mission, currently studying the atmosphere of Mars from orbit, could provide some insight. While its instruments aren’t sensitive enough to detect methane, it can view the chain reactions that could cause methane production as hydrogen escapes the upper atmosphere, causing oxidation at the ground level below.

    Future rovers—or even human explorers—could carry the kind of sensitive instrumentation needed to find the source of the mystery methane spikes. “These results are very exciting, and they're telling us we don't know very much about the atmosphere on Mars,” Webster says.
