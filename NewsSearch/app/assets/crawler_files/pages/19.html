American released by Cuba was wasting away

Alan Gomez and Gregg Zoroya, USA TODAY 11:30 a.m. EST December 17, 2014

The American contractor, who has been held in Cuba on espionage charges for five years, is finally headed home. (News, USA TODAY)

The American contractor at the center of one of the biggest U.S. foreign policy reversals in a half century made clear from a Cuban prison he would not remain a prisoner, even if he let himself waste away.

Alan Gross was released Tuesday after a year of failing health, during which he lost 100 pounds, five teeth and partial vision in one eye. He had promised when he turned 65 in May that he would end his imprisonment "one way or the other."

As he was flown out of Cuba on Wednesday, Gross called family members to tell them he was finally free. On the aircraft were bowls of one his favorite snacks, popcorn, according to media reports.

    Gross, who had just marked five years in prison, was arrested by Cuban authorities on Dec. 3, 2009, while working to improve communications on the island in a contract with the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID).

    The Internet is heavily restricted in Cuba. Gross brought in satellite phones and computer equipment to a small Jewish community on the island.

    USA TODAY

    U.S. seeks to normalize relations with Cuba

    He was charged after his arrest with destabilizing and subverting the government and sentenced to 15 years in prison.

    Cuba had suggested a prisoner swap: Gross for five Cubans imprisoned in the United States, two of whom have since served out their terms. The Obama administration previously had ruled out such an exchange.

    Relatives and politicians visited Gross on several occasions, always urging that he be released. But the Cuban government had steadfastly refused.

    Its resistance stiffened in April, when the Associated Press published a story that uncovered a USAID program aimed at using social media to encourage dissidence in Cuba. It seemed to bolster the Cuban government's concern about covert U.S. operations on the island.

    Gross began a hunger strike that same month, ending it within a few weeks after his 91-year-old mother, Evelyn, pleaded with him to stop. She later died of cancer.

    Gross has two adult daughters, Shira and Nina, according to a website devoted to securing his freedom.

    In recent weeks, as reports emerged that Gross's health was failing and he was being cared for in a prison hospital, the White House repeated demands for his release on humanitarian grounds, saying it was "deeply concerned" about his well-being.

    Earlier this month, on the fifth anniversary of his arrest, his wife, Judy, said "enough is enough. My husband has paid a terrible price for serving his country and community. Alan is resolved that he will not endure another year imprisoned in Cuba, and I am afraid that we are at the end. After five years of literally wasting away, Alan is done."
