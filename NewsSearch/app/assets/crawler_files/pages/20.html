Obama announces talks to normalize diplomatic relations with Cuba

President Obama announced the breakthrough in a speech while Cuban President Raul Castro, the brother of longtime Cuban dictator Fidel Castro, told Cubans he welcomed the restoration of diplomatic ties with the U.S.

BY Dan Friedman
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
Published: Wednesday, December 17, 2014, 11:59 AM
Updated: Wednesday, December 17, 2014, 1:10 PM

The United States will normalize diplomatic relations with Cuba, opening an embassy in Havana and easing trade and travel restrictions in a move that ends more than a half-century of bitter estrangement between the U.S. and the Communist island.

President Obama announced the breakthrough in a noon speech while Cuban President Raul Castro, the brother of longtime Cuban dictator Fidel Castro, spoke in Cuba.

“Today the United States of America is changing its relationship with the people of Cuba,” Obama said.

"We will end an outdated approach that for decades has failed and begin to normalize relations between our two countries," he said.

Speaking in Havana, Raul Castro said: “This decision of President Obama deserves the respect and acknowledgment of our people.”

The deal was facilitated by the release of Alan Gross, 65, an American imprisoned in Cuba since 2009 while working to boost internet access as a subcontractor for the U.S. Agency for International Development. He flew back to the U.S. Wednesday.

Relations with Cuba, once a U.S. colonial possession 90 miles from the coast of Florida, have been suspended since 1961, when Fidel Castro seized power and established a communist dictatorship aligned with the Soviet Union.

The deal came after 18 months of talks hosted by Canada, administration officials said. It was sealed in a conversation Tuesday between Raul Castro and Obama.

The agreement was boosted by a personal appeal by Pope Francis, who wrote letters to Obama and Raul Castro in June pressing them to ease relations. Vatican officials hosted a meeting in Rome and participated in the talks.

"In particular I want to thank his holiness Pope Francis,” Obama said.

The agreement does not end a trade and travel embargo with Cuba, which is regularly renewed by Congress. Tourist travel remains restricted.

“The blockade which causes much human and economic damage to our country should cease,” Raul Castro said in his remarks.

But Obama urged Congress to end the restrictions. Meanwhile administration officials said they are doing what they can within their power to ease limits on trade and travel.

Cuban cigars can now be legally brought the U.S. by someone who legally travels to Cuba and brings back $100 or less worth of tobacco or alcohol for personal consumption.

Obama said the U.S. is lifting limits on remittances that Cubans in the U.S. can send home to family members there.

As part of the deal, Cuba is releasing 53 Cuban prisoners identified as political prisoners by the United States government.

The U.S. sent three Cuban spies imprisoned the U.S. since 1981 back to the island. U.S. officials said the Cuban spies were swapped for a United States intelligence agent who has been in a Cuban prison for nearly 20 years.

Obama called that unnamed spy “one of the most important intelligence agents that the United States ever had in Cuba." He credited the agent with the capture of a network of Cuban spies including the three released Wednesday.

The U.S. is also reviewing its designation of Cuba as a state sponsor of terrorism, the White House said, and is expect to lift the designation quickly.

Though Obama informed key lawmakers of the impending deal, it drew a furious reaction from opponents of liberalizing relations with Cuba.

"President Obama's actions have vindicated the brutal behavior of the Cuban government, "Sen. Foreign Relation Committee Chairman Bob Menendez (D-N.J.), whose parents came from Cuba, said in a statement.

"This is going to do absolutely nothing to further human rights and democracy in Cuba," Sen. Marco Rubio (R-Fla.)— who is weighing a 2016 presidential run and whose parents are also Cuban — told the Huffington Post. "But it potentially goes a long way in providing the economic lift that the Castro regime needs to become permanent fixtures in Cuba for generations to come," Rubio said.

Obama said he respected those critics' views — but he pointed out that isolation has failed to achieve its goal of changing Cuba's leadership.

"I don't believe we can keep doing the same thing for over five decades and expect a different result," he said.

"It does not serve America's interests to try to push Cuba collapse," Obama said.
