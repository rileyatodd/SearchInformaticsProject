Owners of pharmacy tied to deadly outbreak arrested

By John R. Ellement, Kay Lazar and Martin FinucaneGlobe Staff  December 17, 2014

Fourteen people have been charged in the case of a Framingham compounding pharmacy company that has been blamed for sending tainted drugs across the country that killed dozens of people, federal prosecutors said Wednesday.

The defendants linked to the now-shuttered New England Compounding Center Inc., most of whom were arrested in a series of predawn raids, are slated to make their initial appearances in US District Court in Boston Wednesday, officials said.

Continue reading below

The defendants face a 131-count indictment, including charges of racketeering, racketeering conspiracy, introducing adulterated drugs into national commerce, criminal contempt, and mail fraud. The indictment includes allegations that two company officials were responsible for the second-degree murder of 25 patients in seven states.

US Attorney Carmen M. Ortiz said at a news conference at her offices at the courthouse that the case was an “unprecedented national tragedy” that affected over 750 people in 20 different states and killed at least 64 people.

“Production and profit were prioritized over safety,” she said, describing the company as having “filthy conditions” and its labs as “thoroughly contaminated.”

Acting Associate Attorney General Stuart F. Delery, who was one of a host of officials joining Ortiz at the news conference, said, “Let me be clear: actions like the ones alleged in this case display not only a reckless disregard for federal health and safety regulations but also an extreme and appalling disregard for human life.”

“We will never rest in our determination to protect American consumers from harm and to bring justice to victims,” he said. Of the 14 people indicted, 11 have been arrested. The other three were expected to turn themselves in.

The indictment filed in federal court alleges that NECC and Medical Sales Management Inc. — a company that shared ownership with NECC and provided it with sales and administrative services — constituted a criminal “enterprise” under the federal Racketeer Influenced and Corruption Organizations, or RICO, law.

The enterprise’s purpose was to “obtain money and property including through the means of materially false and fraudulent pretenses, representations, and promises,” the indictment said.

WCVB

Founder and owner Barry Cadden (left) was led out of the Wrentham police station in handcuffs. Gregory A. Conigliaro (right) was also taken into custody.

Kimberly Dougherty, an attorney who represents 100 patients and their families who were affected by the fungal meningitis outbreak that arose from the tainted drugs, said outside the courthouse that one of her clients had died an agonizing death two weeks ago.

He was from Michigan and had owned his own auto body shop before getting fungal meningitis. “He didn’t get to see this day,” she said.

She said other clients had been waiting a long time and wondering when this day would come, as they watched the defendants continue to lead “lavish” lifestyles.

Barry Cadden, a founder and owner of the company who also acted as its head pharmacist; and Glenn A. Chin,the supervising pharmacist who oversaw all aspects of production and personnel in the company’s two clean rooms, face 25 allegations of second-degree murder of patients, in Michigan, Tennesse, Indiana, Maryland, Virginia, Florida, and North Carolina.

The murder allegations are listed as “predicate acts” for the racketeering charge. Under the law, prosecutors need to prove two predicate acts to win a conviction under RICO.

Chin already faced a federal mail fraud indictment. He was arraigned in federal court in Boston in September after being arrested several days earlier at Logan International Airport as he and his family were preparing to board a flight to Hong Kong. His lawyer said Chin was not trying to flee the country but was traveling with his family for a vacation.

Four other people also face racketeering charges, though they are not charged with responsibility for the alleged murders.

Gregory A. Conigliaro, another founder and owner of the company, also faces charges. In the indictment, federal prosecutors alleged that he, along with Cadden, deliberately misled federal drug regulators since at least 1989 by falsely identifying the company only as a compounding pharmacy, not a drug manufacturer.

As a drug manufacturer, the company would have faced greater federal scrutiny of its operations.

“We are a small-scale, family-run compounding-only pharmacy,’’ Conigliaro wrote to the US Food and Drug Administration in 2004, according to the indictment. “As such we are not subject to GMP.’’ GMP is an acronym for “good manufacturing practices,’’ which are standards set by the FDA.

Conigliaro and Cadden also allegedly conspired to direct employees to falsify records. In order to operate as a compounding pharmacy, rather than a manufacturer, the company needed names of specific patients. So company officials allegedly instructed workers to create fake patient names. In June 2012, after consulting with Cadden, Conigliaro ordered staff to create 300 fraduulent prescriptions for patients at one Massachusetts hospital, the indictment alleged.

NECC and its related company, MSM, were privately held by Cadden and his wife, and Conigliaro and members of his family, the indictment said.

Conigliaro’s brother, Douglas, and Douglas’s wife, Carla, face multiple counts of criminal contempt for transferring $33.3 million in cash from various bank accounts when US Bankruptcy Court Judge Henry J. Boroff issued an order freezing their assets in early 2013.

In one day alone, authorities allege, the couple transferred $13.5 million from a New York financial institution to two banks, headquartered in Texas and Georgia.

Others charged in the indictment included pharmacists and pharmacy technicians involved in working in the company’s two clean rooms, as well as others involved in the packaging and sale of the drugs.

“This is a complex case. This is a huge case,” said Ortiz. “It’s not the kind of investigation that you just snap your fingers and it’s done. ... In many ways, I have been frustrated by how long it’s taken. But we wanted to make sure we got it right.”

“We will continue, as we have from Day 1, to work incredibly hard ... to make sure that justice is done,” she said.

The move to bring criminal charges came after progress was recently made on the civil litigation spawned by the wave of deaths.

Earlier this month, a proposal filed in federal bankruptcy court called for at least $135 million to be distributed to people who have brought personal injury or death claims against the pharmacy, according to the trustee for the company’s bankruptcy filing and lawyers advising the victims and their families.

Approximately 3,500 parties have filed claims with the bankruptcy court, mostly victims and their family members.
