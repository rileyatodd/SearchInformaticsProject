Feds: Pharmacy used fake names including ‘Coco Puff,’ ‘Donald Trump’

Feds: Pharmacy used fake names including ‘Coco Puff,’ ‘Donald Trump’

Wednesday, December 17, 2014
Print Email Comments

By:

Jennifer Miller

Fake patient names including “Big Baby Jesus,” “Bud Weiser” and “Flash Gordon” were among those used by a Framingham compounding pharmacy facing a slew of criminal charges in federal court today, according to a sweeping indictment.

New England Compounding Pharmacy was licensed only to provide drugs to individual patients with prescriptions, rather than mass-manufacturing drugs.

But “by deceit, craft, trickery and dishonest means,” New England Compounding Center co-founders Barry J. Cadden and Gregory Conigliaro, NECC Director of Operations Sharon P. Carter of Hopkinton, NECC National Sales Director Robert A. Ronzio of North Providence, R.I., and Alla V. Stepanets disguised their illegal drug factory as a simple family-owned pharmacy.

The five are among 14 defendants facing criminal charges including racketeering-second degree murder, conspiracy, contempt and introduction of adulterated drugs into interstate commerce, according to a 73-page indictment issued today. A steroid produced by NECC caused a nationwide fungal meningitis outbreak that killed 64 people and sickened hundreds others in 2012.

The fake names detailed by the feds were used for fraudulent prescriptions for other drugs manufactured by NECC.

As part of the conspiracy, the defendants “used and caused others to use the names of celebrities, fictional characters, doctors and medical staff to create fraudulent prescriptions for those or subsequent orders,” the indictment states.

“Richard Coors,” “Ned Flanders,” “Bob Barker,” “Burt Reynolds,” “Craig Killborne,” “David Letterman, Jay Leno, Jimmy Kimmel” “Dale Earnhardt,” “Fat Albert,” “Robert Redford,” “Bill Clinton,” “Chester Cheeto” “Filet O’fish,” “Long John,” “Jimmy Stewart,” “L.L. Bean,” “Coco Puff,” “Harry Potter” “Ginger Rogers,” “Alex Baldwin, Steven Baldwin, Billy Baldwin,” “Diana Ross,” “Michael Jackson,” “Donald Trump, “Calvin Klein,” “Freddie Mac, Fannie Mae,” “Martha Stewart,” “Samuel Adams,” and “Raymond Rollingrock,” were among the fake patient names used on drugs shipped by NECC.

“Chris Rock” was used repeatedly.

According to the indictment, the defendants made a practice of not including the names of patients on labels affixed to the drugs — allowing the facilities to use the drugs for any patient. And they reused the names of patients to create fraudulent prescriptions, the feds say.

They lied repeatedly to federal officials, according to the indictment.

In response to an FDA inspection of NECC, Conigliaro wrote to the FDA in 2004, “we are a small-scale, family-run, compounding-only pharmacy, not a manufacturer. As such we are not subject to GMP (good manufacturing practices).”

Internally, the company had a rich culture of dishonesty and concealment, the indictment suggests.

In May 2012, the company was under investigation by state regulators after distributing an ineffective anesthetic used in eye surgeries that left patients in pain. The Massachusetts Board of Registration in Pharmacy requested copies of the patient roster — but there was none. According to the indictment, hundreds of doses of the drug had been sold to a Massachusetts hospital with no prescriptions.

Conigliaro then instructed his staff to create 300 fraudulent prescriptions, according to the indictment.

The practice was in keeping with the institution’s procedures, the indictment says.

In an email printed out and hung on the cubicles of NECC’s order-processing staff in May 2012, Cadden and Carter wrote: “All names must resemble ‘real’ names.... no obviously false names! (Mickey Mouse).”

In August 2012, at the end of an email headed “HUUUUUUUUUUGE IMPORTANCE!!!!!!!!!!!!” Ronzio wrote in an email to the staff: “I KNOW I DON’T HAVE TO SAY THIS BUT ALSO NEVER PUT ANYTHING IN WRITING.”
