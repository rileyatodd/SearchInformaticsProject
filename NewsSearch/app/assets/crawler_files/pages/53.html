    Cuba Fund Rallies After Obama Vows to Resume Diplomatic Ties

    By Nikolaj Gammeltoft 2014-12-17T18:08:33Z
    - Comments Email Print Speed
    Share
    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • Google+
    • LinkedIn
    • Email Print
    Save

    Related

    • Obama Says U.S. Moving to Normalize Relations With Cuba
    • Cuba's Castro Urges End to U.S. Embargo (Spanish)

    The Herzfeld Caribbean Basin Fund, one of the only ways to bet on an opening in relations with Cuba, surged to a seven-year high after President Barack Obama pledged to end a diplomatic embargo that lasted for more than 50 years.

    The $45 million fund jumped 35 percent to $9.19 at 12:45 p.m. New York time, the highest since December 2007. Airline Copa Holdings SA and Coke bottler Coca-Cola Femsa SAB, which are among the fund’s biggest positions, rallied at least 2.9 percent. Melia Hotels International SA, which runs 27 hotels in Cuba, surged the most in 18 months.

    The fund consists of companies that stand to benefit when the U.S. lifts its trade restrictions, founder Thomas Herzfeld said in an interview with The Wall Street Journal in 2012. President Obama said the U.S. will initiate talks to resume diplomatic relations, opening a U.S. embassy in Havana and loosening trade and travel restrictions on the nation.

    “Neither the American nor Cuban people are well served by a rigid policy that’s rooted in events that took place before most of us were born,” Obama said today at the White House in a statement that coincided with remarks by Cuban leader Raul Castro in Havana.

    U.S. Exports

    Cuba has sought a shift to gain economic support as its longtime patrons, Russia and Venezuela, have lost influence and been squeezed by plummeting oil prices. U.S. companies will be permitted to export to Cuba telecommunications equipment, agricultural commodities, construction supplies and materials for small businesses. U.S. financial institutions will be allowed to open accounts with Cuban banks.

    Trade with Cuba has been severely restricted for half a century due to a Cold War freeze. The U.S. hasn’t had normal diplomatic relations with Cuba since 1961. The two countries had no diplomatic relations until 1977. Since then, the U.S. has been represented by an interests section within the Switzerland’s embassy to Cuba.

    Under Raul Castro, who took over from his brother, Fidel, as acting president in 2006 and succeeded him two years later, the Marxist nation has taken tentative steps away from central planning. In July, Cuba lowered its economic growth forecast for this year to 1.4 percent.

    Copa advanced 4.7 percent, snapping a 7-day losing streak, to $92.26. American depositary receipts of Coca-Cola Femsa gained 2.9 percent to $86.64, the most since April 16. Melia, the largest western European hotel chain by revenue, rallied 5.6 percent to 8.99 euros. Sherritt International Corp., a producer of nickel in Cuba, rose 23 percent to C$2.79 in Toronto, where the company is based.
