4 Freedom officials indicted for MCHM leak

By Jeff Jenkins in News | December 17, 2014 at 1:40PM

CHARLESTON, W.Va. — Four former officers with Freedom Industries have been indicted by a federal grand jury in connection with the spill of the chemical MCHM into the Elk River on Jan. 9 that created a water emergency impacting 300,000 residents in parts of nine West Virginia counties.

(Read full indictment here)

U.S. Attorney Booth Goodwin said Wednesday afternoon a grand jury in Beckley had indicted former Freedom President Gary Southern, 53, currently of Marco Island, Florida, along with former Freedom owners and officers Dennis P. Farrell, 58, of Charleston, William E. Tis, 60, of Verona, Pennsylvania, and Charles E. Herzing, 63, of McMurray, Pennsylvania.

It’s alleged the defendants failed to operate the tank farm on the Elk River in Charleston in a sound manner and violated the federal Clean Water Act.

Goodwin also announced Freedom environmental consultant Robert Reynolds, 63, of Apex, NC and tank farm plant manager Michael Burdette, 60, of Dunbar, were charged in federal informations. The company, Freedom Industries, was also charged in a federal information. All three allegedly violated the Clean Water Act.

Federal informations usually mean defendants are cooperating with prosecutors.

U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder commented on the indictments in a statement released Wednesday.

“Just a mile and a half upstream from Charleston’s primary source of drinking water, the conditions at the Freedom Industries facility were not only grievously unacceptable, but unlawful. They put an entire population needlessly at risk. As these actions make clear, such conduct cannot, and will not, be tolerated,” Holder said. “These law enforcement actions send an unambiguous message: that compliance with environmental safety standards is an obligation, not a choice. The Department of Justice is committed to vigorously enforcing the Clean Water Act and other natural resource protections. And we will never rest in our efforts to protect the American people — and our environment — from harm.”

“It’s hard to overstate the disruption that results when 300,000 people suddenly lose clean water,” Goodwin said. “This is exactly the kind of scenario that the Clean Water Act is designed to prevent. This spill, which was completely preventable, happened to take place in this district, but it could have happened anywhere. If we don’t want it to happen again, we need to make it crystal clear that those who engage in the kind of criminal behavior that led to this crisis will be held accountable.”

The indictment charges Southern, Farrell, Tis and Herzing with negligent discharge of a pollutant in violation of the Clean Water Act, negligent discharge of refuse matter in violation of the Refuse Act and violating an environmental permit. The charges are in addition to the wire fraud and various bankruptcy fraud charges Southern faces. If convicted of all he faces 68 years in prison. The other defendants could face up to three years behind bars.

The indictment alleges the four men, while corporate officers for Freedom, “approved funding only for those projects that would result in increased business revenue for Freedom, or that were immediately necessary for required equipment maintenance.”

The indictment further alleges the four “failed to take action to fund other repair and upkeep projects” necessary to properly run the elk River facility.

According to the information released by Goodwin, the misconduct alleged in the indictment includes, but is not limited to:

Failure to properly maintain the containment area surrounding the tanks at Freedom’s Elk River facility, and to make necessary repairs to ensure the containment area would contain a chemical spill;

failure to properly inspect a tank containing the chemical MCHM;

failure to develop and implement a spill prevention, control and countermeasures plan;

failure to develop and implement a stormwater pollution prevention plan and groundwater protection plan, both requirements of a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System Permit.

The spill of MCHM prompted a Do Not Use water order being put into effect. The spill happened just a half-mile upstream from West Virginia American Water Company’s Kanawha Valley Plant.
